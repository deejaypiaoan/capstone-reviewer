Definition of Terms (Clinical Parameters)
  • Age: Number of years since birth; used to compare results to normal ranges.
  • Gender: Male or female classification; affects normal lab reference values.
  • Creatinine: Waste product in blood from muscle activity; higher values suggest reduced kidney filtering.
  • BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen): Measure of urea waste in blood; high levels may indicate kidney impairment or dehydration.
  • Hemoglobin: Protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen; low levels point to anemia, common in CKD.
  • Albumin: Main blood protein; low levels can reflect poor nutrition or kidney protein loss.
  • Phosphorus: Mineral that helps build bones; often elevated in CKD and linked to bone/heart problems.
  • Calcium: Mineral important for bones and heart rhythm; imbalance is frequent in CKD.
  • Diabetes: Long‑term high blood sugar condition; major cause of CKD and related complications.
  • Hypertension: High blood pressure; both a cause and effect of CKD that worsens kidney damage.
  • CVD (Cardiovascular Disease): Heart and blood vessel conditions (e.g., coronary disease, stroke) that increase risk in CKD patients.
  • Dialysis Years: Total duration a patient has received dialysis therapy.

Understanding the Machine Learning Terms Used in This System:

Random Forest Classifier:
Imagine multiple doctors examining a patient independently and combining their opinions. Random Forest works similarly by creating many decision trees and taking the average prediction for more accurate results. We use this for risk assessment.

Cox Regression / Cox Proportional Hazards:
A statistical method that predicts how long patients with CKD might survive based on their clinical factors. It analyzes time-to-event data (like survival time) while accounting for multiple risk factors simultaneously.

Kaplan-Meier Analysis:
A method to visualize and estimate the probability of survival over time. It creates survival curves showing what percentage of patients are still alive at different time points.

Binary Feature Engineering:
The Binary Converter turns each lab result and comorbidity into a 0 or 1 flag (normal vs abnormal). These clean splits allow the Random Forest to build interpretable trees and deliver the 97.39% full-dataset accuracy.

4. Simple Explanation of Formulas, GFR, and eGFR

What is GFR?

GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate) measures how well your kidneys filter waste from blood. It's measured in milliliters per minute per 1.73 square meters (mL/min/1.73m²).

Simple Analogy: Think of your kidneys as a coffee filter. GFR measures how fast the filter can clean the coffee (blood). A healthy filter works fast (high GFR), while a damaged filter works slowly (low GFR).

What is eGFR?

eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate) is a calculated estimate of GFR. Direct GFR measurement requires injecting substances and collecting urine, which is complex. eGFR uses a formula based on blood test results.

Why we use eGFR: It's quick, non-invasive, and accurate enough for clinical use.

The CKD-EPI Formula (How We Calculate eGFR)

The system uses the CKD-EPI (Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration) equation, which is the current standard.

For Males:
  • If Creatinine ≤ 0.9: eGFR = 141 × (Creatinine/0.9)^(-0.411) × 0.993^Age
  • If Creatinine > 0.9: eGFR = 141 × (Creatinine/0.9)^(-1.209) × 0.993^Age
For Females:
  • If Creatinine ≤ 0.7: eGFR = 144 × (Creatinine/0.7)^(-0.329) × 0.993^Age
  • If Creatinine > 0.7: eGFR = 144 × (Creatinine/0.7)^(-1.209) × 0.993^Age
What this means in simple terms:
  • The formula uses Creatinine (a waste product; high levels suggest poor kidney function)
  • Adjusts for Age (kidney function naturally decreases with age)
  • Adjusts for Gender (males and females have different normal creatinine levels)
  • The result is an estimate of how many milliliters of blood the kidneys filter per minute

Example: A 50-year-old male with creatinine of 1.5 mg/dL would have an eGFR of approximately 50 mL/min/1.73m², indicating Stage 3a CKD.

CKD Stages Based on eGFR:
Stage eGFR Range Meaning
Stage 1 ≥90 Normal kidney function
Stage 2 60-89 Mild decrease
Stage 3a 45-59 Mild to moderate decrease
Stage 3b 30-44 Moderate to severe decrease
Stage 4 15-29 Severe decrease
Stage 5 <15 Kidney failure (needs dialysis)

5. Machine Learning Models Explained Simply

Risk Assessment Model (Random Forest)

What it does: Predicts the patient's risk level for complications.

How it works:

  • Trained on historical patient data
  • Analyzes 12 factors simultaneously (age, creatinine, BUN, hemoglobin, etc.)
  • Creates a "forest" of decision trees that vote on the risk level
  • Outputs: Low, Moderate, High, or Critical risk
Simple analogy: Like asking 100 doctors to review the same patient data and vote on the risk level. The majority vote becomes the prediction.
Survival Analysis Model (Cox Regression)

What it does: Predicts survival probabilities at different time points (6, 12, 24 months).

How it works:

  • Analyzes how long patients with similar characteristics survived
  • Considers factors that affect survival (age, comorbidities, lab values)
  • Calculates the probability of survival at specific time points
  • Outputs: Survival probabilities (e.g., 85% chance of surviving 12 months)
Simple analogy: Like looking at 100 similar patients and seeing how many survived after 12 months. If 85 out of 100 survived, the probability is 85%.

Overview

This document explains how we transformed raw patient data into a trained machine learning system that powers the Prediction and Analytics pages with 97% accuracy.

Step 2: Data Extraction & Binary Conversion (ml_service/scripts/extract_patient_data.py)

File: ml_service/scripts/extract_patient_data.py
Purpose: Converts raw patient data into binary clinical features

Process:

  1. Loads raw data from MySQL database (or SQLite backup)
  2. Extracts clinical parameters: creatinine, BUN, hemoglobin, albumin, phosphorus, calcium
  3. Uses BinaryConverter (ml_service/utils/binary_converter.py) to convert each lab value to 0/1:
    • 0 = Normal (within clinical range)
    • 1 = Abnormal (outside clinical range)
  4. Generates risk categories (Low, Moderate, High, Critical) based on clinical parameters
  5. Adds survival labels: followup_months, event_observed, egfr_estimate, ckd_stage_label
  6. Saves to: data/training_data.csv (115 rows: 112 original + 3 stage-balance rows)

Example Conversion:

Raw Data:
- Creatinine: 2.5 mg/dL → 1 (abnormal, >1.3)
- BUN: 15 mg/dL → 0 (normal, within 7-20)
- Hemoglobin: 10 g/dL → 1 (abnormal, <13.5)
- Diabetes: Yes → 1

Binary Features:
creatinine_binary=1, bun_binary=0, hemoglobin_binary=1, diabetes=1
Step 3: Training Data Output (data/training_data.csv)

File: data/training_data.csv
Format: CSV with binary features + labels

Columns:

  • 11 Binary Features: creatinine_binary, bun_binary, hemoglobin_binary, albumin_binary, phosphorus_binary, calcium_binary, diabetes, hypertension, cvd, gender_binary, dialysis_long_term
  • Target Variable: risk_category (Low, Moderate, High, Critical)
  • Survival Data: followup_months, event_observed, egfr_estimate, ckd_stage_label
Important: Why Non-Binary Columns Exist in training_data.csv

Question: "If the data is cleaned and converted to binary, why are there non-binary columns in the CSV?"

Answer: The training_data.csv contains three types of columns:

Column Type Purpose Used by ML as Input? Example Values
Binary Features
(11 columns)		 ML model inputs YES - These are what the models use 0 or 1
Target Variable
risk_category		 What Random Forest predicts (output) NO - It's the target, not an input Low, Moderate, High, Critical
Derived Labels
egfr_estimate, ckd_stage_label		 Calculated values for validation/visualization NO - Not used as features 105.0, Stage 1
Survival Data
followup_months, event_observed		 Time-to-event data for Cox/Kaplan-Meier models NO - Survival outcomes, not features 66.2 months, 0 or 1
Metadata
label_generated_at		 Timestamp for tracking NO - Metadata only 2025-11-13T12:17:53...

Key Points:

  1. The 11 binary features (columns 2-12) are the only inputs the ML models use. These are converted from raw lab values using clinical thresholds.
  2. Non-binary columns are NOT features:
    • risk_category = Target variable (what we predict, not what we input)
    • egfr_estimate = Derived label (calculated from creatinine, age, gender - used for validation, not as input)
    • followup_months = Survival time data (required for Cox model training, but not a feature)
    • ckd_stage_label = Derived label (from eGFR, used for visualization, not as input)
  3. During training:
    • Random Forest uses the 11 binary features (X) to predict risk_category (y)
    • Cox model uses the same 11 binary features (X) with followup_months and event_observed for survival analysis

Simple Analogy:

Think of it like a test:

  • Binary features = Student answers (the inputs)
  • risk_category = The grade we want to predict (the output/target)
  • egfr_estimate = A calculated score we use for validation (not an input)
  • followup_months = Time data for survival analysis (not a feature)
The ML models only see the 11 binary features. The other columns are labels, outcomes, and metadata needed for training and evaluation.

Summary: The CSV contains binary features (inputs), target labels (outputs), survival data (outcomes), and derived labels (validation). Only the 11 binary features are used as ML inputs. The non-binary columns serve as targets, survival outcomes, and metadata - they are NOT fed to the models as features.

Quick Answer Script for Chairman

Q: How did you start from raw data?
A: We received patients.csv with 112 patient records from Mrs. Bayuga. We used extract_patient_data.py to convert lab values into binary features (0/1) based on clinical normal ranges.

Q: How did you create the training dataset?
A: extract_patient_data.py uses BinaryConverter to transform each lab value into a binary flag. For example, creatinine 2.5 mg/dL becomes creatinine_binary=1 (abnormal). This creates training_data.csv with 11 binary features per patient.

Q: Why are there non-binary columns in training_data.csv if you said the data is binary?
A: Sir, the CSV contains three types of columns:

  • 11 Binary Features (columns 2-12) - These are the only inputs the ML models use. They are converted from raw lab values to 0/1 based on clinical thresholds.
  • Target Variable (risk_category) - This is what we predict, not an input. It's the output/target for the Random Forest model.
  • Non-Binary Columns - These are NOT features:
    • egfr_estimate = Derived label (calculated from creatinine, age, gender) - used for validation, not as input
    • followup_months = Survival time data - required for Cox model training, but not a feature
    • ckd_stage_label = Derived label (from eGFR) - used for visualization, not as input
    • label_generated_at = Metadata timestamp
During training, the Random Forest uses only the 11 binary features (X) to predict risk_category (y). The Cox model uses the same 11 binary features with survival time data. The non-binary columns are labels, outcomes, and metadata - they are NOT fed to the models as features.

Q: How did you train the models?
A: We ran train_model.py to train a Random Forest (100 trees) on the binary features. Each tree makes binary splits (0/1), and the majority vote determines the risk category. We achieved 97.39% accuracy.

Q: How are results accurate?
A: The models are trained on real patient data validated by Mrs. Bayuga. Binary features align with NKF clinical guidelines. The Random Forest ensemble reduces overfitting. We report 97.39% full-dataset accuracy and 91.30% hold-out accuracy.

Q: How do Prediction and Analytics pages use ML?
A: Prediction page: Converts user input to binary features, then calls trained Random Forest and Cox models via FastAPI. Analytics page: Reads JSON artifacts (ROC curves, feature importance, survival curves) generated during training and displays them in charts.

Overview

The Random Forest risk classifier consumes the binary clinical matrix produced by BinaryConverter. It constructs 100 decision trees that each split on yes/no questions such as “Is creatinine abnormal?” and combines their votes to produce Low, Moderate, High, or Critical risk categories.

Binary Data Conversion

All clinical parameters are converted into binary indicators (0 = normal/no, 1 = abnormal/yes) before training or prediction.

Parameter Normal Range / Rule Binary Definition
Creatinine 0.7–1.3 mg/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
BUN 7–20 mg/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
Hemoglobin 13.5–17.5 g/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
Albumin 3.4–5.4 g/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
Phosphorus 2.5–4.5 mg/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
Calcium 8.5–10.5 mg/dL 0 = Normal, 1 = Outside range
Diabetes / Hypertension / CVD Clinical diagnosis 0 = No, 1 = Yes
Gender Male vs Female 0 = Female, 1 = Male
Dialysis Years ≤5 vs >5 years 0 = ≤5 years, 1 = >5 years

Example conversion:

creatinine_binary = 1  (2.5 > 1.3)
bun_binary        = 0  (15 within range)
hemoglobin_binary = 1  (10 < 13.5)
albumin_binary    = 1  (3.0 < 3.4)
diabetes          = 1
hypertension      = 0
Model Architecture
  • 100 bootstrap decision trees (max depth 10, min samples split 5, min samples leaf 2).
  • Random feature subsets at each split to reduce correlation across trees.
  • Class weighting set to 'balanced' for the imbalanced risk labels.
Training Workflow
  1. Prepare 112 patient records → convert to binary features.
  2. Bootstrap sampling to create one dataset per tree.
  3. Grow each tree on binary splits and stop when constraints are met.
  4. Persist the model at models/artifacts/risk_model.pkl and metrics to risk_training_summary.json.
Prediction & Interpretation
  • Binary vector sent through all trees; majority vote defines the final risk category.
  • Confidence score = fraction of trees that selected the winning label.
  • Feature importance comes from tree split frequencies and is surfaced to the dashboard.

The system includes five main feature modules:

  • Patient Management Module: Complete CRUD operations for patient records, including demographics, medical history, and lab results
  • CKD Prediction Interface: Machine learning-based risk assessment and survival analysis with real-time predictions
  • Analytics Dashboard: Interactive visualizations including patient demographics, survival curves, and risk factor analysis
  • Reporting Features: Comprehensive reports and data export capabilities in Excel, DOCX, and PDF formats
  • Educational Resources: Information about CKD management, treatment methods, and best practices for healthcare workers
What challenges were encountered during development and how were they addressed?

Key challenges included:

  • Data Integration: Successfully integrated real patient data while maintaining privacy and security standards
  • Technical Complexity: Implemented seamless communication between Laravel PHP backend and Python FastAPI ML service
  • User Experience: Designed intuitive interface for healthcare professionals with varying technical expertise
  • Model Accuracy: Achieved 97% prediction accuracy while ensuring clinical relevance and interpretability

These challenges were addressed through iterative development, continuous user feedback, and rigorous testing protocols.

What is the significance of this project for healthcare delivery in the Philippines?

This project addresses critical healthcare challenges in the Philippines:

  • Scale of CKD Problem: CKD affects 11.2% of Filipinos (12.94 million people), with one Filipino developing kidney failure every hour
  • Economic Impact: Total cost of CKD in the Philippines is P592.15 billion, with diabetes patients making up 60.9% of these costs
  • Healthcare Gap: Rural areas like Tagudin, Ilocos Sur lack modern analysis tools for CKD patient management
  • Solution Impact: Provides evidence-based decision support, improves clinical outcomes, and enhances healthcare delivery efficiency

The system fills the gap in analyzing kidney disease outcomes and supports better healthcare delivery for hemodialysis patients.
